The Land of Inspiration



 Iceland's spectacular landscapes, unique culture, and unpredictable weather conditions have always inspired wordsmiths from other countries as well. Viking legends of elves and dwarves formed the basis of Tolkien's epic The Lord of the Rings. The very municipality in the south of Iceland and the glacial valley town of Skagen Fjord are the setting for the events of Ian Fleming's Casino Royale. Europe's largest glacier, Vatnaökul, has become the main setting for the fantasy world created by J.R.R. Martin in Game of Thrones. In 2011, UNESCO recognized Reykjavik, the capital of Iceland, as the City of Literature. Throughout the year, numerous literary events, tours and national monuments have captured Icelanders' love of storytelling.


  Only six other cities in the world have received the title of UNESCO Literary City, and Reykjavik is the first non-English-speaking city among them. In addition to being the birthplace of the Elder Edda, a collection of heroic songs that influenced Scandinavian poetry and ancient Germanic mythology in general, Reykjavík is also famous for its literary festivals. The most famous of them - Reykjavik International Literary Festival - takes place twice a year. Internationally acclaimed authors such as Antonia Byatt and Kurt Vonnegut have read their works here or given interviews.

Iceland, island country located in the North Atlantic Ocean. Lying on the constantly active geologic border between North America and Europe, Iceland is a land of vivid contrasts of climate, geography, and culture. Sparkling glaciers, such as Vatna Glacier (Vatnajökull), Europe’s largest, lie across its ruggedly beautiful mountain ranges; abundant hot geysers provide heat for many of the country’s homes and buildings and allow for hothouse agriculture year-round; and the offshore Gulf Stream provides a surprisingly mild climate for what is one of the northernmost inhabited places on the planet.  Iceland is a Scandinavian country, the world’s oldest democracy but modern in nearly every respect. Unlike most European countries, however, it is ethnically homogeneous, so much so that genetic researchers have used its inhabitants to study hereditary disorders and develop cures for a host of diseases. Although increasingly integrated into the European mainstream, Icelanders take care to preserve their traditions, customs, and language.

The music of Iceland includes vibrant folk and pop traditions, as well as an active classical and contemporary music scene. Well-known artists from Iceland include medieval music group Voces Thules, alternative rock band The Sugarcubes, singers Björk, Hafdís Huld and Emiliana Torrini, post-rock band Sigur Rós, post-metal band Sólstafir, indie folk/indie pop band Of Monsters and Men, blues/rock band Kaleo, metal band Skálmöld and techno-industrial band Hatari. Iceland's traditional music is related to Nordic music forms. Although Iceland has a very small population, it is home to many famous and praised bands and musicians.
